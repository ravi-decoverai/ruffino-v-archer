Ruffino v. Archer is a medical malpractice case arising from the treatment of John Ruffino, a 56-year-old truck driver, at TriStar StoneCrest Medical Center in Smyrna, Tennessee on February 17, 2016. The case centers on the failure to administer intravenous tissue plasminogen activator (IV tPA) and the failure to arrange transfer to a comprehensive stroke center for endovascular thrombectomy following an acute ischemic stroke caused by complete occlusion of the proximal left middle cerebral artery (MCA) M1 segment.

Mr. Ruffino arrived at StoneCrest by ambulance at 0948 after experiencing dizziness and speech difficulty while driving. He was found to be neurologically normal during six serial neurological assessments between 1000 and 1200. New neurological deficits—slurred speech, right-sided facial weakness, dysphasia, and right arm weakness—were first identified at approximately 1220 when the attending ER physician, Dr. Clark Archer, examined him. Despite these findings, no tPA was administered and no transfer was arranged for several hours. Mr. Ruffino was eventually transferred to Centennial Medical Center at 2250 with a deteriorating neurological status (NIHSS 13, up from 4). He was left with significant cognitive and physical disabilities.

Key Disputes

Central Question

Did the failure to administer IV tPA and transfer John Ruffino to a comprehensive stroke center for endovascular thrombectomy fall below the standard of care, and did this cause a worse neurological outcome?

Plaintiffs' Position (Pope, Callahan, Dhar)

  • Mr. Ruffino was neurologically normal from at least 0949 through 1200. Any symptoms by 0830 were TIAs, not a stroke.
  • The severe stroke did not manifest until at or after 1200. When Dr. Archer identified new deficits at ~1220, they had been present less than one hour.
  • Dr. Archer was required to initiate IV tPA and arrange transfer to a comprehensive stroke center for thrombectomy.
  • Dr. Archer and Nurse Bromley fell below the standard of care by not effectively communicating to Dr. Chitturi that the patient was neurologically normal from 1000–1200.
  • Had treatment been provided within 6–8 hours of 1220, Mr. Ruffino would have had a better outcome. CTA confirmed a clot suitable for thrombectomy.

Defendants' Position (Jarquin-Valdivia, Dodds, Chitturi)

  • Mr. Ruffino's left MCA was already chronically occluded or 99% stenosed since at least December 2015.
  • He woke up with symptoms, making "last known normal" when he went to bed the night before — well beyond the 3-hour tPA window by arrival at 0948.
  • Even if tPA had been given, it would not have opened a chronically occluded vessel. 84.5% of M1 patients had poor outcomes despite tPA.
  • NIHSS of 4 made him ineligible for endovascular thrombectomy per AHA/ASA guidelines (requires NIHSS ≥ 6).
  • Stenting was not indicated per the 2011 SAMMPRIS trial. The care at StoneCrest met the standard of care.

Medical History

Mr. Ruffino began experiencing intermittent transient neurological events in December 2015, including right arm numbness, heaviness, dizziness, and sudden speech arrest. His first episode occurred at WalMart on December 15, 2015. He was referred to neurologist Dr. Deka Efobi by Dr. Luck on December 14, 2015 and diagnosed with TIAs and vertebrobasilar syndrome.

An MRA and MRI performed on December 23, 2015 at University Medical Center were read as normal by radiologist Dr. Matthew Bernhard. However, defense experts later found the MCA was already occluded or 99% stenosed on those same images. Dr. Efobi prescribed gabapentin for possible seizures. On a follow-up visit February 11, 2016, Mr. Ruffino reported continuing "mini strokes." An EEG was normal.

Events of February 17, 2016

While driving for work, Mr. Ruffino experienced episodes between 0800–0830. His boss called 911. EMS reported speech slightly slurred. He arrived at StoneCrest ER at 0948 and was triaged at 0956 with no neurological deficits observed. He was neurologically normal from 1000–1200 per six serial neuro checks. New deficits appeared between 1200–1300. NIHSS was 4 at StoneCrest, deteriorating to 13 by transfer to Centennial at 2250.

Outcome

  • CT perfusion at Centennial (Feb 18) showed large left MCA stroke
  • Discharged Feb 26 on clopidogrel 75mg, aspirin, atorvastatin 40mg, gabapentin 300mg, lisinopril 20mg
  • Re-admitted Feb 27 after fall at home; NIHSS worsened to 21
  • MRI Feb 28: deep left hemisphere stroke, no new restricted diffusion
  • Discharged Mar 3 to St. Thomas Midtown Rehab
  • Left with significant cognitive and physical disabilities
